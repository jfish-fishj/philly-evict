\section{Overview}\label{overview}

This document reports an event study of eviction filing rates around
ownership transfers in Philadelphia rental properties.

The annual analysis now uses a \textbf{full building-year panel} over
calendar years 2006--2019. Treatment is a building's \textbf{only
observed sale} in the RTT data within that window; controls are
buildings \textbf{never sold} in RTT during the same window. We track
treated buildings from 3 years before to 5 years after that sale, set
all event-time dummies to zero for never-sold controls, and estimate
building fixed effects models with common calendar-year shocks.

\section{Data Construction}\label{data-construction}

\subsection{Transfer events}\label{transfer-events}

Transfer events come from the Philadelphia Real Estate Transfer Tax
(RTT) records (\texttt{rtt\_clean}), which log every deed transfer with
buyer (grantee), seller (grantor), sale price, and document type.

\textbf{Processing steps:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Multiple transfers of the same PID in the same year are collapsed to
  one observation, keeping the transfer with the highest
  \texttt{total\_consideration}.
\item
  \textbf{Sheriff's deeds} (foreclosures) are flagged via
  \texttt{document\_type}. Approximately 10\% of all RTT records are
  sheriff's deeds. These are predominantly received by banks, servicers,
  and government-sponsored enterprises (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, HUD)
  --- entities that temporarily hold title after foreclosure, not active
  landlords.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Owner linkage}\label{owner-linkage}

Buyer names are standardized and linked via a two-stage pipeline:

\textbf{Stage 1 --- Entity grouping} (\texttt{r/build-owner-linkage.R}):

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Corporate entities} are classified using
  \texttt{business\_regex} (matching LLC, INC, CORP, TRUST, BANK, and
  \textasciitilde40 other business keywords). Legal entity suffixes
  (LLC, INC, CORP, LP, LTD, CO, COMPANY) are stripped to group variants
  (e.g., ``EMC MORTGAGE CORPORATION'' and ``EMC MORTGAGE LLC'' map to
  the same owner group).
\item
  \textbf{Individual names} are \emph{not} grouped across transfers ---
  matching ``SMITH ROBERT'' across properties without address
  verification would create false merges. For 10+ unit buildings, 97\%
  of top-evicting transfers are corporate, so this primarily affects
  small single-family properties where portfolio analysis is less
  relevant.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Stage 2 --- Conglomerate formation}
(\texttt{r/build-ownership-panel.R}):

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Entity groups are merged into \textbf{conglomerates} via shared
  mailing addresses (Phase 2), transitive RTT grantee chains (Phase 3),
  and PA Dept of State business filing records (Phase 4). Phase 4 is the
  critical addition: it links shell company networks where legally
  distinct LLCs share a registered agent or officer but have completely
  different names and addresses.
\item
  \textbf{Portfolio size} is computed at the \textbf{conglomerate} level
  from non-sheriff acquisitions, so a landlord operating 10 shell LLCs
  is counted as a 10-property operator, not 10 separate single-purchase
  entities. Bins: Single-purchase, 2--4, 5--9, 10+, and Sheriff-only.
\item
  \textbf{Acquirer filing rate} is also computed at the conglomerate
  level: for each transfer, we look at all other PIDs in the same
  conglomerate in years before the transfer (using
  \texttt{xwalk\_pid\_conglomerate}), not just the PIDs acquired by that
  specific entity.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Event panel}\label{event-panel}

For the annual design, we restrict the sample window to 2006--2019, keep
treated PIDs with \textbf{exactly one observed transfer} in that window,
and construct an event-time window \(t \in \{-3, \ldots, +5\}\) around
that sale. Buildings never sold in RTT during 2006--2019 remain in the
panel as controls with all event-time indicators set to zero. We merge
building-level outcomes from \texttt{bldg\_panel\_blp}:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Filing rate}: \(\text{num\_filings} / \text{total\_units}\)
  (contemporaneous, not the EB-smoothed pre-COVID rate)
\item
  \textbf{Violations}, \textbf{complaints}, \textbf{rent} as secondary
  outcomes
\item
  \textbf{Rental flags}: \texttt{rental\_from\_license},
  \texttt{rental\_from\_altos}, \texttt{rental\_from\_evict},
  \texttt{ever\_rental\_any\_year\_ever}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Sample:} 2,598,476 building-year observations in the full
baseline regression (after singleton removal). Among treated buildings,
observed outcome coverage rises from 41.6\% at \(t = -3\) to 42.9\% at
\(t = +5\). All annual regressions restrict to calendar years
2006--2019.

The single-transfer restriction is now the default. Relative to keeping
all treated PIDs' first sale, it keeps 156,039 treated buildings and
drops 79,636 repeat-transfer treated buildings, which would otherwise
contribute 571,040 building-year rows. The dropped repeat-transfer
properties are slightly more corporate (36.6\% vs.~22.2\%), somewhat
larger (1.4 vs.~1.6 units), and have lower sale prices (\$73,000
vs.~\$116,000).

\section{Specification}\label{specification}

The baseline event study estimates:

\[
\text{FilingRate}_{it} = \sum_{k \neq -2} \beta_k \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{EventTime}_{it} = k] + \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{it}
\]

where \(i\) indexes buildings (PIDs), \(t\) indexes calendar years, and
EventTime \(k \in \{-3, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}\) relative to the
building's \textbf{single observed transfer year} in the 2006--2019 RTT
window (with \(k = -2\) as the omitted reference period). For never-sold
controls, all event-time indicators equal zero in every year.
\(\alpha_i\) are building fixed effects and \(\gamma_t\) are year fixed
effects. Standard errors are clustered at the PID level.

The building FE absorb time-invariant building characteristics (size,

to 22.8\% are observed with rental evidence only after the sale.

As a functional-form check, we also re-estimate the same baseline and
buyer-type specifications using a Poisson event-study with outcome
\texttt{\textbackslash{}text\{num\textbackslash{}\_filings\}\_\{it\}}
and offset
\texttt{\textbackslash{}log(\textbackslash{}text\{total\textbackslash{}\_units\}\_\{it\})}.
This keeps zero-filing building-years in sample while modeling filing
intensity on the count scale with unit exposure absorbed by the offset.

We do \textbf{not} stack multiple transfer events for the same PID in
the main annual design. Instead, treatment is limited to PIDs with
exactly one observed RTT transfer in 2006--2019, and controls are PIDs
with no RTT transfer in that same window.

\section{Heterogeneity by Acquirer Filing
Rate}\label{heterogeneity-by-acquirer-filing-rate}

We classify acquirers by their \textbf{pre-acquisition temporal filing
rate} on the acquirer's \emph{other} properties in years before the
transfer. For each transfer of building \(i\) by conglomerate \(j\) in
year \(Y\), we pool the other buildings' pre-\(Y\) filing counts and
unit counts, then compute a \textbf{unit-weighted} filing rate: \[
\text{AcqRate}_{jY} = \frac{\sum_{p \neq i,\, t < Y} \text{num\_filings}_{pt}}{\sum_{p \neq i,\, t < Y} \text{total\_units}_{pt}}.
\] This avoids giving a single-family house the same weight as a large
apartment building, and it avoids look-ahead bias because the
classification uses no post-transfer data.

Critically, the classification is now at the \textbf{conglomerate} level
(post-Phase 4 matching). This means a landlord who made an acquisition
via shell LLC ``A'' is classified using the pre-acquisition filing
behavior of all other LLCs in the same conglomerate --- including shell
companies ``B'', ``C'', and ``D'' that were linked to the same operator
via PA business filing records. Under entity-level matching, each shell
LLC would be classified as a single-purchase acquirer; under
conglomerate-level matching, they are correctly identified as portfolio
operators.

Acquirers are classified into four bins:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{High-filer portfolio (10+ other units)}: Unit-weighted
  pre-acquisition filing rate above 0.050 filings per unit-year among
  portfolios with at least 10 other units
\item
  \textbf{Low-filer portfolio (10+ other units)}: At or below that
  threshold among the same meaningful portfolios, including zero-filing
  operators
\item
  \textbf{Small portfolio (\textless10 other units)}: Has some other
  properties, but fewer than 10 other units at acquisition
\item
  \textbf{Single-purchase}: No other properties in the conglomerate's
  history
\end{itemize}

The high/low cutoff is fixed at \textbf{0.050 filings per unit-year}
(approximately 64.3th percentile of the positive-rate
meaningful-portfolio distribution). Using a fixed threshold rather than
the sample median makes the classification invariant to sample
composition and has a direct economic interpretation (roughly 5 eviction
filings per 100 units per year).

\subsection{Stacked DiD: Filing Rate
Heterogeneity}\label{sec-stacked-did}

To address the concern that post-acquisition filing changes reflect
general time trends rather than causal acquisition effects, we implement
a stacked difference-in-differences design. For each acquisition cohort
\(g\) (transfer year), we pair treated buildings with a clean control
group of buildings that were \textbf{never transferred} over 2006--2019,
then stack cohort-specific sub-panels. The estimating equation is:

\[
\text{FilingRate}_{it} = \sum_{k \neq -1} \beta_k \cdot \bigl(\text{Treated}_i \times \mathbf{1}[t - g_i = k]\bigr) + \alpha_{ig} + \delta_{tg} + \varepsilon_{it}
\]

where \(\alpha_{ig}\) is a building \(\times\) cohort fixed effect
(absorbs the treated/control level difference within each cohort's
panel), \(\delta_{tg}\) is a calendar-year \(\times\) cohort fixed
effect (absorbs common time trends within each cohort), and \(t=-1\) is
the reference period. Standard errors are clustered by building. The
event window is \(k \in \{-3,\ldots,+3\}\) and the sample is restricted
to calendar years 2006--2019. Controls appear once per cohort in the
stacked dataset; clustering by raw PID across all cohort copies is
conservative (treats repeated control observations as one cluster).

The \textbf{treated} group in each specification is buildings acquired
by a given acquirer type; the \textbf{control} group is always buildings
matching the same size threshold that were never transferred. Crucially,
``10+ units'' refers to the \textbf{acquired building's own unit count}
(not the acquirer's portfolio size) --- this isolates the effect on
large apartment buildings regardless of how the portfolio-size
classification was derived. We report five specifications:

\subsection{No sheriff's deeds}\label{no-sheriffs-deeds}

Sheriff's deed transfers are foreclosures, where the ``buyer'' is
typically a bank or GSE (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac) that temporarily holds
title. These entities do not operate as landlords and are unlikely to
file evictions. Excluding sheriff's deeds removes approximately 10\% of
transfers.

\subsection{Allow repeat-transfer
PIDs}\label{allow-repeat-transfer-pids}

The baseline now defaults to the single-transfer sample. As a robustness
check, we re-introduce repeat-transfer PIDs by using each building's
first observed sale in the analysis window even when that PID later
sells again.

\subsection{No nearby re-transfer}\label{no-nearby-re-transfer}

We exclude transfers where the same PID has another transfer within 2
years, avoiding contamination from overlapping ownership spells.

\subsection{Count outcome}\label{count-outcome}

Using the raw count of eviction filings (\texttt{num\_filings}) instead
of the rate (per unit) confirms the direction of the effect. This
addresses the concern that rate-based measures might be sensitive to
changes in \texttt{total\_units} around transfers.

\subsection{Any-filing indicator}\label{any-filing-indicator}

A binary indicator for whether any eviction filings occurred in a given
year tests whether the effect operates on the extensive margin (more
buildings filing) rather than only the intensive margin (more filings
per building).

\subsection{Violations outcome}\label{violations-outcome}

Building code violations are examined as a secondary outcome.
\textbf{Note:} Philadelphia L\&I violations data begins in 2007, so the
violations regressions are additionally restricted to calendar years
\(\geq\) 2007 to avoid spurious zeros in the pre-period.

\section{Appendix: Continuous Acquirer Filing Rate
Interaction}\label{appendix-continuous-acquirer-filing-rate-interaction}

The event-study heterogeneity analysis in the main text bins acquirers
by pre-acquisition filing rate. As a complementary specification, we
pool the high- and low-filer bins into a single ``portfolio acquirer''
subsample (2+ unit buildings, acquirer has \(\geq\) 10 other units at
transfer) and treat the pre-acquisition filing rate as a continuous
moderator. The estimating equation is:

\[
\text{FilingRate}_{it} = \sum_{k \neq -2} \beta_k \cdot \mathbf{1}[t_i = k] \times \widetilde{\text{AcqRate}}_i + \alpha_i + \alpha_t + \varepsilon_{it}
\]

where
\(\widetilde{\text{AcqRate}}_i = \text{AcqRate}_i - \bar{\text{AcqRate}}^w\)
is the demeaned pre-acquisition filing rate, \(\bar{\text{AcqRate}}^w\)
is the unit-weighted mean across acquired buildings (one observation per
PID, weighted by building size, matching the regression weights), and
the sum runs over \(k \in [-3, +5] \setminus \{-2\}\). This is the same
filing-rate measure that determines placement into the high-filer bin
(threshold: 0.05 filings/unit-year).

\subsection{Pooled post-treatment DiD}\label{pooled-post-treatment-did}

As a more parsimonious summary, we collapse the event study into a
single post-treatment indicator:

\[
\text{FilingRate}_{it} = \gamma_0 \cdot \text{Post}_{it} + \gamma_1 \cdot \text{Post}_{it} \times \widetilde{\text{AcqRate}}_i + \alpha_i + \alpha_t + \varepsilon_{it}
\]

where \(\text{Post}_{it} = 1\) for \(t \geq 1\) (post-transfer), 0 for
\(t < 0\) and for never-sold controls. The transfer year (\(t = 0\)) is
excluded. The pooled DiD column appears as column (2) in the table
above.

Neither the level effect (\(\hat{\gamma}_0\)) nor the heterogeneity
interaction (\(\hat{\gamma}_1\)) is significant. This confirms that the
dynamic binned patterns are not masking a detectable continuous gradient
in post-transfer filing intensity with respect to acquirer
