\section{Overview}\label{overview}

This document reports an event study of eviction filing rates around
ownership transfers in Philadelphia rental properties. The central
question: \textbf{do eviction rates change when a property changes
hands, and does it depend on who buys it?}

We construct a panel centered on each transfer event, tracking filing
rates from 3 years before to 5 years after the sale. We estimate
building fixed effects models that identify the effect from
within-building variation over time, controlling for year shocks.

\textbf{Key findings:}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  Eviction filing rates increase sharply after ownership transfers,
  rising by approximately 0.8 percentage points (relative to a baseline
  of \textasciitilde3\%) within two years of sale.
\item
  The effect is driven entirely by \textbf{corporate buyers} ---
  individual-to-individual transfers show no increase or a slight
  decline in filings.
\item
  The effect is \textbf{not limited to small properties} --- it appears
  across all building sizes, with the largest point estimates for 10--19
  and 20+ unit buildings.
\item
  Larger portfolio buyers (10+ properties) show the steepest increases.
\item
  \textbf{Selection concern}: Corporate transfers substantially increase
  the probability that a property obtains a rental license (+9 pp at
  \(t+1\)) or appears in rental listings (+8 pp at \(t+0\)). This
  suggests corporate buyers are bringing formerly owner-occupied
  properties onto the rental market, mechanically expanding the
  population at risk for eviction.
\item
  \textbf{Acquirer filing history matters}: Using pre-acquisition filing
  rates on the acquirer's other properties (years before the transfer),
  high-filer portfolio acquirers show the largest post-transfer
  increase; low-filer portfolio acquirers show a moderate increase;
  non-filers and single-purchase acquirers show little change or a
  decline.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Data Construction}\label{data-construction}

\subsection{Transfer events}\label{transfer-events}

Transfer events come from the Philadelphia Real Estate Transfer Tax
(RTT) records (\texttt{rtt\_clean}), which log every deed transfer with
buyer (grantee), seller (grantor), sale price, and document type.

\textbf{Processing steps:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Multiple transfers of the same PID in the same year are collapsed to
  one observation, keeping the transfer with the highest
  \texttt{total\_consideration}.
\item
  \textbf{Sheriff's deeds} (foreclosures) are flagged via
  \texttt{document\_type}. Approximately 10\% of all RTT records are
  sheriff's deeds. These are predominantly received by banks, servicers,
  and government-sponsored enterprises (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, HUD)
  --- entities that temporarily hold title after foreclosure, not active
  landlords.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Owner linkage}\label{owner-linkage}

Buyer names are standardized and linked via
\texttt{r/build-owner-linkage.R}:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Corporate entities} are classified using
  \texttt{business\_regex} (matching LLC, INC, CORP, TRUST, BANK, and
  \textasciitilde40 other business keywords). Legal entity suffixes
  (LLC, INC, CORP, LP, LTD, CO, COMPANY) are stripped to group variants
  (e.g., ``EMC MORTGAGE CORPORATION'' and ``EMC MORTGAGE LLC'' map to
  the same owner group).
\item
  \textbf{Individual names} are \emph{not} grouped across transfers ---
  matching ``SMITH ROBERT'' across properties without address
  verification would create false merges. For 10+ unit buildings, 97\%
  of top-evicting transfers are corporate, so this primarily affects
  small single-family properties where portfolio analysis is less
  relevant.
\item
  \textbf{Portfolio size} is computed from non-sheriff acquisitions
  only, so that Fannie Mae's 1,200 foreclosure takeovers do not inflate
  its portfolio bin. Bins: Single-purchase, 2--4, 5--9, 10+, and
  Sheriff-only.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Event panel}\label{event-panel}

For each transfer, we construct an event-time window
\(t \in \{-3, \ldots, +5\}\) years relative to the sale year. We merge
building-level outcomes from \texttt{bldg\_panel\_blp}:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Filing rate}: \(\text{num\_filings} / \text{total\_units}\)
  (contemporaneous, not the EB-smoothed pre-COVID rate)
\item
  \textbf{Violations}, \textbf{complaints}, \textbf{rent} as secondary
  outcomes
\item
  \textbf{Rental flags}: \texttt{rental\_from\_license},
  \texttt{rental\_from\_altos}, \texttt{rental\_from\_evict},
  \texttt{ever\_rental\_any\_year\_ever}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Sample:} 1,471,948 building \(\times\) event-time observations
in the full baseline regression (after singleton removal). Coverage
increases from 27\% at \(t = -3\) to 38\% at \(t = +5\), reflecting the
panel's forward expansion. All regressions restrict to pre-COVID
observations (calendar year \(\leq\) 2019).

\section{Specification}\label{specification}

The baseline event study estimates:

\[
\text{FilingRate}_{it} = \sum_{k \neq -1} \beta_k \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{EventTime}_{it} = k] + \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{it}
\]

where \(i\) indexes buildings (PIDs), \(t\) indexes calendar years, and
EventTime \(k \in \{-3, -2, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}\) relative to the
transfer year (with \(k = -1\) as the omitted reference period).
\(\alpha_i\) are building fixed effects and \(\gamma_t\) are year fixed
effects. Standard errors are clustered at the PID level.

The building FE absorb time-invariant building characteristics (size,
location, quality). The progressive sample restrictions address key
identification concerns: \textbf{2+ units} excludes single-family homes
where a single filing produces a mechanically large filing rate;
\textbf{pre-transfer rental} restricts to buildings that had rental
evidence (rental license, Altos/Apartments.com listing, or eviction
filing) in any year \emph{before} the transfer. This is critical: 21\%
of transfer-events involve properties that first appear as rentals only
\emph{after} the sale (potential owner-occupied-to-rental conversions).
Using ``ever rental'' (pre or post) would contaminate the sample with
these conversions, where filing rate increases may be mechanical rather
than behavioral. The pre-transfer restriction ensures we study
properties that were already operating as rentals.

For \textbf{overlapping event windows} (49\% of PIDs have multiple
transfers), we stack all transfer events. Single-transfer-only is a
robustness check.

\textbf{Full sample} (Spec 1): Filing rates increase post-transfer,
sustained through \(t + 5\). Pre-period coefficients at \(t - 3\) and
\(t - 2\) are negative (relative to \(t = -1\)), consistent with an

specification --- established rentals where filing rate increases cannot
be explained by rental market entry.

\tightlist

data. All rental entry regressions are restricted to year \(\geq\) 2011.
Altos (Apartments.com) listing data is available from 2006.

where most properties are owner-occupied (not at risk for

only (calendar year \(\leq\) 2019).

\section{Heterogeneity by Acquirer Filing
Rate}\label{heterogeneity-by-acquirer-filing-rate}

We classify acquirers by their \textbf{pre-acquisition temporal filing
rate} on the acquirer's \emph{other} properties in years before the
transfer. For each transfer of building \(i\) by owner \(j\) in year
\(Y\), we compute the mean filing rate
(\(\text{num\_filings} / \text{total\_units}\)) across all other PIDs
acquired by owner \(j\), using only years strictly before \(Y\). This
avoids look-ahead bias --- the classification uses no post-transfer
data.

Acquirers are classified into four bins:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{High-filer portfolio}: Above-median pre-acquisition filing

\item
  \textbf{Low-filer portfolio}: At or below median pre-acquisition
  filing rate
\item
  \textbf{Non-filer (has portfolio)}: Zero filing rate but owns multiple
  properties
\item
  \textbf{Single-purchase}: No other properties in the data (cannot
  compute a portfolio filing rate)
\end{itemize}

\section{Robustness}\label{robustness}

building panel is merged with transfer timing, and explicit event-time
dummies are constructed for \(k \in \{-3, \ldots, +5\}\) (omitting

zero, providing natural within-PID control periods. This approach avoids
the problem of counting every observation as ``treated'' that would
arise from a stacked event panel. All robustness specs restrict to 2+

\subsection{No sheriff's deeds}\label{no-sheriffs-deeds}

Sheriff's deed transfers are foreclosures, where the ``buyer'' is
typically a bank or GSE (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac) that temporarily holds
title. These entities do not operate as landlords and are unlikely to
file evictions. Excluding sheriff's deeds removes approximately 10\% of
transfers.

\subsection{Single-transfer PIDs}\label{single-transfer-pids}

The baseline allows overlapping event windows for PIDs with multiple
transfers. As a robustness check, we restrict to PIDs with only one
transfer during the sample period.

\subsection{No nearby re-transfer}\label{no-nearby-re-transfer}

We exclude transfers where the same PID has another transfer within 2
years, avoiding contamination from overlapping ownership spells.

\subsection{Count outcome}\label{count-outcome}

Using the raw count of eviction filings (\texttt{num\_filings}) instead
of the rate (per unit) confirms the direction of the effect. This
addresses the concern that rate-based measures might be sensitive to
changes in \texttt{total\_units} around transfers.

\subsection{Any-filing indicator}\label{any-filing-indicator}

A binary indicator for whether any eviction filings occurred in a given
year tests whether the effect operates on the extensive margin (more
buildings filing) rather than only the intensive margin (more filings
per building).

\subsection{Violations outcome}\label{violations-outcome}

Building code violations are examined as a secondary outcome.
\textbf{Note:} Philadelphia L\&I violations data begins in 2007, so the
violations regressions are additionally restricted to calendar years
\(\geq\) 2007 to avoid spurious zeros in the pre-period.
